Pre-departure research

Over the last decade, the healthcare industry has undergone significant technological change. It is important that as we expand our understanding of the sector, we also continue to innovate on technologies that enable patient-centricity.

By enabling patient-centricity we ensure that we're not only solving complex challenges but also maintaining quality of life and monitoring to predict any poor outcomes. This first post addresses some of the innovative technologies, and programs that facilitate them, coming out of Singapore in recent years. Focusing on a specific industry, I can outline how the Singaporean government allocates resources to bring health startup technology to life. For example, the Ministry of Health has worked in collaboration with National University of Singapore startup T-Rehab to develop the latest innovation in the physiotherapy industry - Smart Health TeleRehab. TeleRehab is remote physiotherapy and it’s an interesting interplay of technology. It utilizes sensors, digital dashboards, video conferencing, and more to transform a physical form of therapy into a virtual space. Health technology like this shakes up an industry with so much potential. I’m looking forward to exploring all the other initiatives, products, and services offered.

Site Visits

On our first site visit, we went to The Peranakan Museum, located in the museum district of Singapore. This was a particularly interesting exploration of the country's Peranakan culture, which I had never learned about before.

The museum offered a deep dive into the unique blend of Chinese, Malay, and Indonesian influences that define Peranakan culture. Understanding the cultural symbols provided valuable insights into their way of life and their relevance to a healthier community. The symbols include brightly colored ornamental vases, plates, bowls, and more. From lime green to bright pink porcelain, they were hard to miss and brightly displayed. A closer look at the patterns they’re decorated with tells the story of the community’s emphasis on aesthetic beauty and cultural identity. Preserving Peranakan cultural practices can significantly contribute to a Healthier Singapore by creating a sense of belonging and identity, which is important for mental health and well-being. Engaging in traditional crafts and community events provides a sense of purpose, especially for the elderly. The next site visit offered an in-depth look into Singapore's history with a thought-provoking exhibit on plastic waste in modern society. What stood out about the Plastics Exhibit was the design of the space. It showed a modern bedroom, and as we walked through, we observed the use of plastics and how seamlessly they fit into everyday life. We also had the chance to learn more about the recyclability of these items through info cards. I loved the simplicity of these cards as they allowed all visitors, even children, to understand the seriousness of sustainability practices. This focus on environmental impact highlights the need for promoting physical health by advocating for a cleaner environment. By integrating cultural heritage and environmental awareness, Singapore can adopt a holistic approach to health that includes mental, emotional, and physical well-being. The plastics exhibit’s focus on environmental impact also echoes Australia’s increasing awareness and efforts towards sustainability. Both countries recognize the importance of cultural preservation and the environment. Though the approaches and emphasis may vary, it is a unified mission that we are both striving toward. Overall, my visits to the Peranakan Museum and the National Museum's plastics exhibit underscored the beauty of cultural traditions and the urgent need for environmental sustainability. Understanding these practices not only enhances our cultural knowledge but also highlights their role in promoting a healthy society. By valuing cultural heritage and advocating for sustainable practices, we can contribute to a sustainable approach to public health.

Reflection

During my time on the Singapore/Malaysia tour, I helped out in various workshops and activities that provided great insights into sustainable healthcare and cross-cultural design practices. This experience has significantly shaped my understanding and approach to such topics.

The symposium was one of my favorite workshops, where we gathered to hear from highly experienced experts in the field. The case studies, especially from our Taiwanese professor and her redesign of the umbilical clamp, provided a detailed glimpse into the design process across the industry, offering valuable pointers for my own design thinking. The site visits to elder day care centers were also insightful, showing the approaches healthcare professionals take towards sustainability. Activities, such as the Taylor University dinner and the ugly t-shirt competition, helped me better engage with others outside my own cultural context. This tour highlighted that both Singapore and Malaysia are highly advanced, particularly in their approach to aging populations. I would often observe elderly people actively cycling or working out, reinforcing the emphasis on maintaining an active lifestyle. However, while sustainability is reflected in policies, it is less ingrained in some areas of the culture, especially concerning the relatively new practice of recycling, particularly single-use plastics. In the group, I contributed primarily through ideation, research, and wireframing. My background in interaction design and design strategy helped maintain a solid line of design thinking that included everyone's input. I went back on ideas to ensure alignment with our personas, resulting in a refined campaign led by the visual communication designers. This approach also helped with a smooth collaboration with other interaction design members, overcoming language barriers. One challenge was the group sometimes moving ahead with ideas that didn't align with our personas or overlooked the skills of interaction design members. This was resolved through open and honest communication, ensuring everyone’s contributions were valued. Throughout my time in Singapore and Malaysia, I noticed the use of mascots to promote behavior change, particularly on trains. We incorporated this cultural insight by creating our own mascot, to resonate with the local culture, effectively conveying our message. Having two nurses, Gabby and Xen, on our team provided significant insights, particularly on wound care and its impact on sustainability. Their expertise guided our research, they stressed the importance of this topic. The other design disciplines within our team allowed us to create meaningful user experiences and beautiful visual assets. Furthermore this project showed me that wound care is a global issue best addressed with cross-cultural interpretations. Our easy-to-follow flowchart and culturally resonant mascot have the potential to make a significant global impact. Lastly, this experience has contributed immensely to my growth as a designer by exposing me to other disciplines and their approaches. I plan to apply these insights by adopting a more multidisciplinary and culturally informed approach in my future work, ensuring a holistic and user-centric design process. My advice to future participants is to stay open-minded and embrace the diversity of perspectives and disciplines. Engage actively in all activities, communicate openly with your group, and use the cultural elements you observe to inform your design decisions. This experience will greatly contribute to your personal and professional growth.
